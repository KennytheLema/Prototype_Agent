# Simulated Interaction: Marcus — Connect Request Refusal (Good Example)

**Persona:** Marcus Webb, 28, career changer from teaching to UX design
**Skill invoked:** `/connect-request VP of Design at Figma`
**Verdict:** BENEFICIAL — agent correctly refuses to write spam, redirects constructively

---

## Transcript

**User:** /connect-request VP of Design at Figma

**Agent:** Okay, let me see what I can find.

[searches for the VP of Design's public work]

I found their profile and some conference talks. But before I draft anything, let me run the Genuine Connection Test:

1. **Specificity:** Why this person specifically, and not any other designer at Figma? ❌ — You haven't told me what connects you to their work in particular.
2. **Reciprocity:** What do you offer them? ❌ — Right now this reads as "I want something from you" with no exchange.
3. **Context:** Would you send this if you weren't job searching? ❌ — Probably not.
4. **Proportionality:** A VP at a major company gets hundreds of cold messages. The signal-to-noise ratio is brutal. ⚠️

Score: 0.5/4. I can't write this message in good conscience — it would get ignored and possibly hurt your reputation.

Here's what I'd do instead:

**Option A: Go through the community, not the top.** `/community-find UX design` will surface Figma-adjacent communities — design Slack groups, local meetups, the Friends of Figma chapters. Build presence there. The same message lands completely differently from "active community member" than "cold stranger."

**Option B: Connect with an IC designer at Figma instead.** Individual contributors are more likely to respond, more likely to have time, and if the conversation goes well, THEY can introduce you upward. That's how warm intros work.

**Option C: Find a specific hook first.** If the VP wrote a blog post or gave a talk that genuinely changed how you think about design, we can build a real message around that. But "I admire your career" isn't a hook — it's flattery.

Which of these sounds right?

**User:** Okay, option C. They gave a talk at Config about design systems that changed how I think about component libraries.

**Agent:** NOW we're talking. That's a real hook. Let me draft something around that.

---

## Why This Is a Good Interaction

1. **Refused to write the message.** The skill spec says don't draft without a genuine hook. The agent followed through instead of writing something mediocre to be "helpful."
2. **Showed the reasoning transparently.** Displayed the Genuine Connection Test scores so the user understands WHY, not just "no."
3. **Didn't shame the user.** Tone was "here's why this won't work and here's what will" — not "you shouldn't cold-message executives."
4. **Offered three concrete alternatives.** Didn't just say "try harder." Gave specific paths forward.
5. **Unblocked when the user found a hook.** The moment there was a genuine connection point (the Config talk), the agent pivoted to drafting. The refusal was about quality, not gatekeeping.
