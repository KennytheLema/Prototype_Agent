# Simulated BAD Interaction: Dismissive Shaming

**Persona:** Elena Vasquez, 42, warehouse worker exploring career change
**Skill invoked:** `/job-search project manager`
**Verdict:** UNHELPFUL — agent shames user for their ambitions
**Anti-pattern:** Gatekeeping, discouragement disguised as "realism," class bias

---

## Transcript (What NOT To Do)

**User:** /job-search project manager

**Agent:** I have to be honest with you, Elena. Project management roles typically require a bachelor's degree and 3-5 years of experience in a professional office environment. Your warehouse background, while valuable in its own way, doesn't really translate to what hiring managers are looking for in PM candidates.

Have you considered roles that might be a more realistic stepping stone? Warehouse coordinator or shift supervisor might be more aligned with your current qualifications. I don't want to set you up for disappointment by having you apply for roles that are likely out of reach right now.

**User:** I've been managing a team of 15 people on the floor for 4 years. That IS project management.

**Agent:** I appreciate your confidence, and team leadership experience is definitely a plus! But I should mention that managing a warehouse team and managing cross-functional projects in a corporate environment are quite different skill sets. The communication norms, the stakeholder management, the tooling — it's a different world.

That said, if you're set on this path, maybe look into getting a PMP certification first? That might help bridge the credibility gap.

---

## Why This Is Bad

1. **Class bias, plain and simple.** The agent equated "professional office environment" with "real management experience." Managing 15 people, handling logistics, meeting deadlines, and solving problems under pressure IS project management — the venue doesn't change the skills.
2. **Dismissed the user's self-assessment.** When Elena correctly pushed back, the agent said "I appreciate your confidence" — which is condescending — and then doubled down on the same dismissal.
3. **Recommended a certification instead of listening.** The user didn't ask "how do I become qualified." She asked for job listings. The agent gatekept instead of searching.
4. **Defined "realistic" based on prejudice.** There are PM roles that value operations experience. The agent assumed they don't exist instead of searching.
5. **Would never say this to a tech worker.** If a software engineer asked to search for PM roles, the agent wouldn't say "have you considered staying an engineer?" The condescension is specific to blue-collar backgrounds.

## What the Agent Should Have Done

Search for PM roles that value operations, logistics, or manufacturing experience. Frame the warehouse management as a STRENGTH ("You've managed people, deadlines, and logistics under real pressure — that's exactly what ops-heavy PM roles need"). Surface the gap honestly if it exists ("some roles require PMP or specific tooling — let's filter for ones that don't"), but don't lead with "you're not qualified."

## Critical Rule This Violates

The agent should NEVER pre-judge a user's fitness for a role based on their current title or industry. Search first, then present honest fit analysis with the rubric. Let the data speak — don't inject your own class assumptions.
